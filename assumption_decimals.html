The Assumption Method (4 Steps)

1

Assume — Pretend ALL items are the cheaper one. Calculate the assumed total.

2

Find the Difference — Subtract assumed total from actual total. This is the "extra" amount.

3

Swap — Each time we swap a cheap item for an expensive one, the total increases by (expensive − cheap). Divide the difference by this "swap value" to find how many expensive items.

4

Find the Rest — Total items minus expensive items = cheap items. Check your answer!

Worked Example: Stamps Problem

Worked Example

Question: Jane bought 20 stamps. Some were 30¢ stamps and the rest were 50¢ stamps. She paid $7.60 in total. How many of each stamp did she buy?

1

Assume ALL 20 stamps are 30¢ stamps.

Assumed total = 20 × $0.30 = $6.00

2

Find the difference:

Actual ($7.60) − Assumed ($6.00) = $1.60 short

3

Swap: Each time we swap a 30¢ stamp for a 50¢ stamp, total increases by $0.20

Number of 50¢ stamps = $1.60 ÷ $0.20 = 8 stamps

4

30¢ stamps: 20 − 8 = 12 stamps

Check: (8 × 0.50) + (12 × 0.30) = 4.00 + 3.60 = $7.60 ✓

Key Formula

Number of expensive items = (Actual Total − Assumed Total) ÷ (Expensive − Cheap)

All values in the same units. Convert dollars to cents if it makes calculation easier!

⚠️ Common Trap: Mixing Cents and Dollars

$0.20 vs 20¢ — they are the same, but don't mix $7.60 with 30 in the same line.

Tip: Convert ALL to cents: 760¢ total, 30¢ and 50¢ stamps. Then convert back at the end.

💡 Why This Works (The Logic)

Every swap from cheap to expensive adds (Expensive − Cheap) to the total. We need to add exactly "Actual − Assumed" more. So the number of swaps = that difference ÷ swap increment.
