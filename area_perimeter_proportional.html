The Golden Rule: Dimension vs. Area

This is the #1 most tested pitfall in P4 area problems:

ChangePerimeterArea
Length × 2× 2 (doubles)× 2 (doubles)
Breadth × 3× (changes)× 3 (triples)
Both × 2× 2 (doubles)× 4 (quadruples!)
Both × 3× 3 (triples)× 9 (9×!)

Area is 2-dimensional: if both length AND breadth scale by n, area scales by n².

Type 1: One Dimension is a Multiple

Worked Example

Question: A rectangle has length 12 cm. Its breadth is 1/3 of its length. Find its area and perimeter.

1

Breadth = 1/3 × 12 = 4 cm

Ratio: L : B = 3 : 1

2

Area = 12 × 4 = 48 cm²

Perimeter = 2 × (12 + 4) = 32 cm

Type 2: Rectangle Enlarged (Both Dimensions)

Worked Example

Question: Rectangle A measures 5 cm by 3 cm. Rectangle B has dimensions twice those of A. How many times is the area of B compared to A?

1

Rectangle A: Area = 5 × 3 = 15 cm²

2

Rectangle B: Length = 5 × 2 = 10 cm, Breadth = 3 × 2 = 6 cm

Area = 10 × 6 = 60 cm²

3

Ratio: 60 ÷ 15 = 4 times

Shortcut: Both dimensions × 2 → Area × 2² = × 4 ✓

Type 3: Scaling Squares

Quick Pattern
1

Small square: side 2 cm → Area = 4 cm²

Big square: side 6 cm (3×) → Area = 36 cm²

Ratio: 36 ÷ 4 = 9 times (= 3²)

2

For squares: If side ratio is a : b, then area ratio is a² : b²

Side ratio 2:5 → Area ratio 4:25

⚠️ The #1 Trap: Linear Thinking for Area

"If both sides double, area doubles too" — WRONG!

Area is 2D. Both × 2 → Area × 4. Both × 3 → Area × 9. Always square the scale factor.
