What it is

Multiplying and dividing decimals by whole numbers: treat it like a whole number operation, then place the decimal point. For multiplication: count decimal places in the question — the answer has the same number. For division: the decimal point in the quotient sits directly above the decimal point in the dividend.

Multiply Decimal by Whole Number

Example: 2.34 × 3 = ?
1

Ignore the decimal — multiply as whole numbers: 234 × 3 = 702

2

Count decimal places in question: 2.34 has 2 decimal places. Put 2 in answer.

2.34 × 3 = 7.02

💡 Teaching tip

Use estimation to place the decimal: 2.34 × 3 ≈ 2 × 3 = 6. So the answer must be 7.02, not 70.2. Estimation catches decimal point errors instantly.

Divide Decimal by Whole Number

Example: 6.28 ÷ 4 = ?
1

Set up long division. Place the decimal point in the quotient directly above the dividend's decimal point.

    1.57
  ─────
4 ) 6.28
2

Work through: 6÷4=1 r2, bring down 2 → 22÷4=5 r2, bring down 8 → 28÷4=7.

    1.57
  ─────
4 ) 6.28

⚠️ Common trap

Students forget to place the decimal point in the quotient and write 157. The visual cue "dot above dot" must become muscle memory. Drill this explicitly.

Remainders — Express as Decimals

Example: 5 ÷ 4 = ?

5 ÷ 4 = 1 remainder 1. To express as a decimal: add a zero and decimal point → 5.0 ÷ 4 = 1.25

💡 Teaching tip

Write 5 as 5.0 to make the decimal explicit. Students often stop at "1 remainder 1" — train them to continue dividing by adding zeros.

🔮 Beyond MOE P4 — Dividing by a Decimal

This content is NOT required by MOE for P4. Included for GEP, advanced learners, and P5 preparation.

The Key Rule: Make the Divisor a Whole Number

Rule: Multiply BOTH numbers by 10, 100, etc. until the divisor becomes a whole number. This doesn't change the answer.

Example: 4.2 ÷ 0.3 = ?
1

Divisor 0.3 has 1 decimal place. Multiply both by 10:

4.2 ÷ 0.3 = 42 ÷ 3
2

Now divide as normal: 42 ÷ 3 = 14

Another: 0.48 ÷ 0.06 → multiply by 100: 48 ÷ 6 = 8

💡 Teaching tip

Think of it as "how many 0.3s in 4.2?" — the same way you'd ask "how many 3s in 42?" Multiplying both by 10 is like converting cents to dollars. Frame it as "getting rid of the decimal" to make it intuitive.
